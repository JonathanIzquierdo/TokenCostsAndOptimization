Manual Coding is Dead—Long Live Software Engineering
An opinionated article on artificial intelligence in the software development lifecycle. As such, take it with a grain of salt.🧂

Executive Summary
The writing is on the wall. AI can code software faster and more efficiently than a human. It’s just a question of learning to instruct it. The AI of today is the worst it will ever be. Stop coding manually, this is the biggest revolution in software engineering ever!

Call to Action
Manual coding is dead. Dead, dead, dead. 💀. Finito. Toast. Kaput. It’s gone to meet its maker. Kicked the bucket. Pushing up the daisies.

There’s a lot of software engineering to be done. Our customers won’t vibe-coding anything of importance.

Instead of coding, you should spend 95+% of your time understanding the problem domain, specifying the problem in the form of requirements and technical plans, instructing the AI, reviewing its output, and ensuring the AI can do its job.

AI is the first tool of choice, not the last.

Manual Coding Died in 2025
I dipped my toes into AI in the spring of 2024 on a long train trip from Oulu to Helsinki. Absolutely floored by what AI could even then accomplish, even though it was essentially just generating small snippets of code. But it showed a path to something new. Later, agentic mode came out with the AI iterating on its own output, “thinking” itself to a solution. In 2025, the models improved. Now, they are awesome. There is nothing the AI can’t write. It’s just a matter of instructing it properly. Manual coding is dead.

While leading the GitHub Copilot masterclass initiative during the last year, I’ve heard various objections to using AI. Let’s visit them.

AI is Not Smart Enough
Sure, it’s not perfect. Maybe it’ll never be. Can it take your legacy monolith in an exotic programming language with a zillion intricate implicit domain rules and still develop at breakneck speed? Probably not. But there are many success stories globally and at Visma about its merits. We are talking about 30x speedups in some technical migration cases. 30x, not a typo: where we thought it’d take months to accomplish, we experimented with the AI for a few days, and then it ran the final migrations in a few hours.

AI might never surpass human judgment, and we will need to help it in the big monoliths and other confusing environments. That’s OK. The AI of today is the worst it will ever be, and so far, it’s evolving at an incredible rate.

AI is Expensive
If there’s one thing I’d want people in a workplace to realize is how incredibly valuable their time is. As a rule of thumb, an engineer costs €15.000 euros per month, or €100/hour. Tools costing tens or even hundreds of bucks per month which can multiply your productivity are a no-brainer to use. It’s incredible when you think about it: most established industries would sell their own grandmother for a 10% across the board productivity/profit improvement. Here we are talking about technologies reaching for 1000% impact, and still, people are hesitant to jump on the maglev train. It’s about to leave the station, you know. Don’t get left out standing at the platform.

Onboarding is Hard/Clumsy, I Don’t Know Where to Start
There’s a lot to learn, and the landscape keeps on changing. It’s understandably stressful, and there is a fear of the unknown. We from the AI Engineering and Technology team are here to help.

For example, it can suggest checking out our GitHub Copilot masterclass for learning the bits and pieces of your AI coding assistant. Or focus on AI in testing in the new workshop. Or accelerate your technical migration in the modernization workshop. Most importantly, start. See AI as the first resort, not the last.

But I’m Better than AI at Programming!
I know you are better than AI. That’s not the point. The AI is good enough. And it's faster, and doesn’t tire out. To me, this is very similar to the discussion in the 1980s. C compilers were introduced, and the assembler die-hards had similar comments: the C compilers were buggy, slow, and produced bad code; they could do it much better and faster themselves in assembly. And they were absolutely right! In the next years, the C compilers completely took over, producing code much faster and more correctly than a human—a true leap in productivity. And yes, there are still assembler gurus who outsmart compilers. So what?
A similar jump occurred again when we moved to managed runtimes such as Java and C#. Today, most people can’t even read assembler, let alone find a bug in it. Each leap in productivity does imply we lose something; that’s inevitable. Using AI will mean we lose some understanding. Now, we’re in a liminal phase where we need to be experts in both traditional programming technologies and in commanding the AI. That’s a tall order, but the reality at the moment: the skill requirements for a software engineer have increased due to AI, not decreased. Having to review a lot of AI-generated code isn’t for the faint of heart either.

AI Does Not Make Me As Productive
Quite so. Every new technology comes with a learning curve. AI is revolutionizing software development. Such a huge enabler of productivity improvement does not come in the form of minor changes to your existing work habits. It’s not surprising that utilizing AI well requires dedicated effort to improve. Initially, there will be a productivity dip. You’ll need to learn to voice your inner thoughts to the AI. The good news is that you know what to say already—you have had this inner monologue when programming! But it takes practice to master, of course.

AI is Coming, We’ll All Be Fired
Frankly, I haven’t fully understood the logic behind this statement. If you go to your company’s management and say, “Hey, I’m 10x more productive than previously”, what are they going to say? “Let’s fire the guy, he’s too good?“ Rubbish.

AI is changing roles, just like technical progress always has. So what does it bode for programmers? Some doomsayers predict a gloomy future. I don’t. When given the choice between more software and fewer programmers during the last 40 years, the answer has always been more software in the long run. So I’m not too worried, and a recent Lenny’s Newsletter on the State of the product job market in early 2026 suggests that the demand for AI roles overall is exploding. Embracing AI is a CV-safe move and opens up new doors for you in the future. Sure, the intrinsic activity of programming will be different.

But Programming is Fun!
Yes, it is. It’s one of the best things ever, right? And I bet many of us yearn for the time in our young adult years when we could just dive into the zone with the Emacs editor, a glass of whiskey in front of a fireplace. That cozy feeling of hacking fantastic programs, character by character, alone but at peace. I’d like to think it looked like this:



Me, in the year 2000. Minus the fireplace and the Laphroigh, I was too poor. Ok, I didn’t have the laptop either. But the Emacs part is true.



So what happened? AI made writing code manually obsolete. The AI is removing the fun part, right?

I thought so too, at least initially. But I also found out something else about the circumstances and about myself. Writing code was never the point; solving problems was. This is still true today. Somewhat inexplicably, I take as much joy today in solving a problem with AI as I did previously without AI. No, shoot. Even more joy, because I can accomplish more, faster, without stupid typos, missing semicolons, or a hundred other details that really were irrelevant to the problem. The AI does it all a lot faster. As Frederick Brooks said, we build “castles in the air, from air, creating by exertion of the imagination”. I like to focus on building castles rather than on individual bricks.

What To Do
The AI can write anything; it’s mostly about how to instruct it properly. But, putting thoughts into words and steering others or other things isn’t what our zone has been about. This is the crux of the issue. I don’t have the mental flashback of being in a zone telling others what to do, because it was never a zone for me; that came later in management roles. All that telling others was happening in my head, because I planned the software myself and wrote it myself. So, to some extent, many of us feel like juniors in front of this revolutionary technology, and we are not used to writing out our thought process. That’s understandable.

Primarily, becoming AI-native is about overcoming the mental block that we should be coding manually, and being genuinely interested in the possibilities that AI brings us.

I’d like to hammer down two tenets:

Stop coding manually. Really. 95+% of the code should be created by AI. What happens when you stop coding manually? You will hit rocks and trees on that journey, i.e., you will fail to instruct the AI. That’s where growth begins.
I could go on about other things. When you fail, learn to use custom instructions. You’ll quickly learn they can be too restrictive. Hence, the skills system. Teaching the AI to validate its own work is fundamental: if it can iterate on its own work, running tests and course-correcting itself (the plan-do-check loop), you’re not just doubling productivity. You’re on the path to 10x:ing yourself. And so on. Context engineering is a discipline of its own, and we are all learning here.

But all that is just pie-in-the-sky nonsense if you don’t begin with the first step: Stop. Coding. Manually.

There’s a lot of software engineering to be done. Our customers will not be able to vibecode anything significant on their own. Programming was, and is, about taking vague, ever-changing requirements and distilling them into logical, technical systems of actions and records, with a sane conceptual model for future expansion. AI does not force you to make good decisions regarding, e.g., security, logging, or maintainability. Only a few percent in any given population are capable of this, and you’re one of them. (Congratulations; it’s a fantastic job!)

AI is the new future, and it’s scary, but you are well-equipped to learn it and take advantage of it.
